Removal of tooth and wisdom tooth

Wisdom tooth removal is the most horrible dental procedure in most people's mind.

We want to prove that this is in fact not true.

First of all, the surgery is in the hands of a specialist of experience and specific qualification, who has performed a large number of operations of this kind, therefore this requires a fraction of the treatment time. Not only surgical time but also the quality and amount of the expected complications (swelling, pain) are closely related to professional execution.

On the other hand, modern medicine has a special focus on painless treatments. In the simplest cases, a special injection of the local anaesthetic solution provides complete painlessness.

 If any of the following reasons, inflammation, increased fear, raises the need for more serious assistance, we can perform the procedure by adding medication and carry out the operation in alert sedation (narcotization). In this case, the patient will be supervised by an anesthesiologist during the entire duration of the surgery.

It is possible to remove wisdom teeth one by one but also combined, in one sitting. The severity and consequences depend on the level of intervention.

The patient is informed about the course and details of the procedure in words and written form as well.

Bone Replacement

The fundamental condition of implantation is that the bone is of sufficient size and it is capable to accommodate the implant completely. If this condition is not met due to previous bone loss, bone replacement may be required prior to or at the same time as the implantation process.

At the area of the jaw that remained toothless for a long time after tooth extraction, especially if the patient is wearing removable denture or where the teeth have been removed for reasons of periodontology, the bone my degrade to such an extent that it becomes impossible to implant artificial root. No matter what condition we are facing, there are almost aren’t any cases where we cannot help. There are several ways to rebuild the missing bone. The nature of the planned replacement, the size and the exact location of bone defect determines which solution we choose.

In the simplest case, the so-called guided bone regeneration method is used to create a suitable position to receive implants using bone replacement material and membrane. This can be done in the area of the extracted tooth, in the area of the sinus and at the same time as the implantation.

In the case of more extensive deficits, we remove an adequate amount of bone from the jaw bone or hip and attach the sufficient amount to the area of deficit so the bone will be able to accommodate the implant. (see below: bone transplantation)

see In some cases, we can replace the deficiency with the patient’s bone carved in 3D, perfectly in order to match the size of the missing part. (see: high-tech surgery)

The type of anaesthetic used depends on the severity of the procedure. It is not the pain but rather the long surgical time, due to precise execution, that justifies the assistance of an anaesthesiologist. However, for bone transplantation, full narcosis is usually the best option.

Bone Substitutes

In some cases of bone deficits, bone regeneration can be achieved through the use of bone replacement material. These substances fill the area of the deficit and maintain the desired volume until the area is populated by bone-forming cells and form bone tissue.

Membranes

The membranes are used in guided tissue regeneration (bone replacement) to keep the granular bone replacement substances in place and protect the area that regenerates beneath them. Except for rare exceptions, we use a membrane that is usually absorbed.

Sinus Lift

One of the safest ways to resupply the bone is by filling the area of the bone that was occupied by the sinus after tooth extraction, with bone replacement material. This ensures that we can treat bone deficiency of the lateral part of the upper jaw bone safely, allowing the placement of an implant of sufficient length.

Bone Transplantation

In case of severe bone deficiency, the "gold standard" of bone replacements is the replacement of one’s own bone. In the case of bone widening, it is often sufficient to use a block of bone removed from the area of the jaw behind the lower teeth, but often the hipbone is the only part that can supply enough bone material that allows implantation later. These procedures usually require hospitalisation and anaesthesia, but despite the worrying description, the majority of patients’ experience is positive. The discomfort associated with surgery is not related to pain, but rather to the temporary replacement of teeth. However, we often find a solution for that, which is well suited to everyday life.
