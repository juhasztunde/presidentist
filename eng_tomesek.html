Fillings

Surgery and dental restoration

Aesthetic filling (white filling, composite/glass ionomer filling)

Nowadays it is generally accepted that the amalgam filling, used previously, is out of date. Therefore, in our office, we use a material which is chosen according to the purpose and the size of the filling, but one that is always tooth-coloured. During filling, after the removal of the cavity, the remaining socket is filled with a tooth-coloured filling material which, in its elasticity and wear resistance, is very similar to the material of the tooth. This allows us to restore the tooth as close to the original in appearance and function as possible.

Inserts (Inlay, Onlay, Overlay)

A well-made aesthetic filling created from excellent material is the basic pillar of modern dentistry. Therefore it can fulfil its duty for quite some time. What could be a reason for exchange then? White materials (composites) suffer a different amount of shrinkage when hardened by light. Due to shrinkage, the edge of the filling can diverge from the tooth, therefore, a secondary decay can occur. You can’t tell how much time this process can take. It depends on the quality of the filler that was used, from the precision of medical work, from the cleanness of the mouth and the patient’s tendency to tooth decay. On average, a “white” filling should function well for 5-7 years.

The following symptoms can be warning signs that it is time for an exchange: sensitivity to sweet food, cold or discoloured filling. In this case, we can fix the problem by removing the old filling, cleaning the cavity and making a new one. This will always result in a loss of teeth material, so the cavity will become deeper, more extensive with time. An average person should have his filling changed at least 4 to 5 times during his life as a young adult to ensure that the repaired tooth will no longer decay.

There is a way to preserve the tooth permanently and stop the filling changes. It is possible by using inserts or inlays, which is not a new technology at all.

This technique was used since the heroic age of dentistry and the material used was gold. The dentist develops a cavity in the tooth according to well-defined principles afterwards a precision impression is taken. The technician will prepare the filling, which will be inserted into the tooth during the next treatment. After this, shrinkage will not occur, so the chances of secondary decay are minimized. With proper hygienic care of the tooth, there is no need to renew the filling anymore. Modern technology now allows the use of not only gold but also tooth-coloured material. This can be used to restore a tooth in an invisible way to the untrained eye, with extremely high aesthetic results. Another huge advantage is that it is the only reliable, long-term method of restoring strongly-destructed teeth by preserving as many intact teeth as possible.

The most outstanding aesthetically valued inserts can be made from high ceramic-filled composites or presses.
